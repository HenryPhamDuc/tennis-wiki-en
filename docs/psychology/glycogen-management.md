# Glycogen Management

**Glycogen Management** is the strategic fuelling of the body's primary carbohydrate energy store — muscle and liver glycogen — to ensure maximum explosive capacity is available from the first point to the last. Glycogen is the fuel for the [glycolytic energy system](../technique/atp-pc-system-and-energy-systems.md) and the primary resynthesis substrate for the ATP-PC system. When it depletes, explosive performance collapses — first in the legs, then throughout the entire athletic system.

> *"Optimal glycogen management is about ensuring that the athlete's 'gas tank' is full before the race, being topped up during the race, and being efficiently refuelled afterwards."*

---

## Why Glycogen Is the Critical Fuel

The three energy systems in order of intensity:
1. ATP-PC (immediate, 0–10s) — refuelled by phosphocreatine and aerobic metabolism
2. Glycolytic (anaerobic, 10–90s) — fuelled by **glycogen and glucose**
3. Aerobic (sustained, >2 min) — fuelled by glycogen, fat, protein

Glycogen sits at the intersection of systems 2 and 3. A glycogen-depleted player:
- Cannot sustain glycolytic output for extended rallies
- Shifts to fat oxidation (aerobic) — which is 40% slower at converting fuel to ATP
- Loses explosive first-step speed as the ATP-PC replenishment pathway slows
- Experiences "heavy legs" — the subjective signal of glycogen-depleted quadriceps

In a 3-hour match at 25°C, an elite player can burn **1,500–2,500 kcal**, with carbohydrate contributing 60–70% of total energy expenditure. Without active glycogen management, depletion begins in the second set.

## The Three Phases

### Phase 1: Pre-Match Loading (24–48 hours before)

**The Goal:** Fill glycogen stores to maximum capacity.

- **Carbohydrate Intake:** 7–10g per kg of bodyweight per day, for 24–48 hours pre-match
- **Food Sources:** Rice, pasta, potatoes, bread, oats — complex carbohydrates that absorb at a sustained rate
- **Pre-Match Meal (3–4 hours before):** Low glycaemic index (GI) carbohydrates + moderate protein + minimal fat and fibre (to avoid GI distress); e.g., oatmeal + banana + chicken

**The Glycogen Supercompensation protocol:** After a training session that partially depletes glycogen, the body upregulates glycogen synthase — increasing its capacity to store glycogen. Consuming high-GI carbohydrates immediately after this depletion allows supercompensation, filling glycogen stores to 10–20% above baseline levels.

### Phase 2: During-Match Maintenance

**The Goal:** Maintain blood glucose and slow glycogen depletion rate.

- **Every 45–60 minutes:** 30–60g of carbohydrates (1–2 gels, a banana, or a glucose-electrolyte drink)
- **Changeovers:** Primary window for carbohydrate intake — the 90-second changeover allows digestion to begin without GI distress during play
- **Blood Glucose:** A drop in blood glucose mid-match triggers hypoglycaemia — dizziness, cognitive slowing, loss of shot selection. This is separate from glycogen depletion and is managed by the frequent, small carbohydrate doses during play

**The Glucose-Electrolyte Drink:** Simultaneously addresses glycogen maintenance AND [hydration](../players/hydration-and-electrolytes.md). At 4–8% carbohydrate concentration, it empties from the stomach faster than pure water and delivers glucose directly to the bloodstream.

### Phase 3: Post-Match Recovery — The Golden Window

**The Goal:** Maximise glycogen resynthesis rate.

The **30-minute Golden Window** immediately after exercise is when:
1. Muscle cell membranes are maximally permeable to glucose
2. Glycogen synthase activity peaks
3. Protein synthesis is elevated (muscle repair begins)

**Protocol:**
- **Carbohydrates:** 1.0–1.5g per kg of bodyweight immediately post-match
- **Protein:** 20–40g of fast-absorbing protein (whey isolate or whole food equivalent) within the same 30-minute window
- **Ratio:** 3:1 or 4:1 carbohydrate-to-protein (e.g., 60–90g carbohydrates + 20–30g protein)

Missing the Golden Window and eating 2 hours post-match reduces glycogen resynthesis rate by approximately **50%**, significantly impairing next-day readiness — critical during tournament play with consecutive match days.

## Tournament-Week Glycogen Strategy

| Day | Glycogen Protocol |
|---|---|
| Match Day (Day 1) | Loading (7–10g/kg carbs) + during-match maintenance + Golden Window |
| Day After Match | Moderate carbs (5–7g/kg); focus on protein for repair |
| Day Before Next Match | Re-loading begins; 7–10g/kg carbs |
| Next Match Day | Same as Match Day |

During Grand Slam weeks with matches every 2 days, glycogen loading becomes continuous — players cannot afford to allow full glycogen depletion between matches.

## Glycogen and Cognitive Function

An often-overlooked consequence of glycogen depletion is **cognitive decline**. The brain runs almost exclusively on glucose. When liver glycogen (the brain's primary fuel source) depletes:
- Decision-making speed slows
- Risk assessment degrades — the player starts going for lower-percentage shots
- Emotional regulation weakens — the first signs of [Self 1 intrusion](self-1-vs-self-2.md) under pressure often coincide with blood glucose drops

This is why [Between-Point Ritual](between-point-ritual.md) execution quality drops late in matches: the neurological substrate that powers prefrontal cortex function (impulse control, tactical planning) is becoming glycogen-depleted.
